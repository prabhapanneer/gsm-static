AIF, PMS & SIF

A simple explanation of more specialised investment routes such as AIF, PMS, and SIF, how they differ, and how GSM looks at suitability before discussing them.

Why these routes exist

Not every investor is looking for the same kind of investment route.

\n

Some investors are comfortable with regular mutual funds. Some may want a more customised portfolio. Some may want access to more specialised strategies. That is where AIF, PMS, and SIF may come into the picture.

\n

These are not default options for everyone. They are more specialised routes and should be looked at only when they truly fit the investor’s profile, risk comfort, liquidity needs, and overall financial plan.

\n

What is PMS?

PMS means Portfolio Management Services.

In simple terms, PMS is a more customised way of managing investments. Instead of joining a common mutual fund pool in the usual way, the portfolio is managed under an agreed strategy for the client.

What is AIF?

AIF means Alternative Investment Fund.

In simple terms, an AIF is a pooled investment route that follows a defined strategy outside the usual mutual fund route. Depending on the category and strategy, an AIF may invest in areas such as private businesses, private credit, infrastructure, special situations, or other alternative opportunities.

What is SIF?

SIF means Specialized Investment Fund.

\n

In simple terms, SIF is a more specialised route for investors who want strategies beyond regular mutual funds, while still staying closer to a fund-based investment framework.

\n

A simple way to understand the difference

PMS is closer to a customised portfolio route. AIF is closer to a pooled alternative route. SIF is closer to a specialised strategy route that sits between regular products and more complex structures.

\n

None of these should be chosen just because they sound premium. The real question is whether the structure, risk, liquidity, cost, and holding period actually suit the investor.

\n

AIF categories in simple terms

AIF is not one single bucket. It is broadly divided into three categories.

\n
Category I Usually includes funds that invest in areas such as startups, early-stage businesses, social ventures, infrastructure, or sectors considered economically useful.
Category II Usually includes funds such as private equity, private credit, or other closed-ended strategies that are more structured and less trading-oriented.
Category III Usually includes funds that may use more market-linked and trading-oriented strategies, and in some cases can take both long and short exposure depending on the structure.
Why this matters Different categories can mean very different types of risk, liquidity, time horizon, and investment approach.

How GSM approaches AIF, PMS & SIF

At GSM, we do not present these as default or glamorous options.

\n

We first try to understand whether this type of route is suitable at all. We look at the investor’s financial position, current portfolio, liquidity needs, time horizon, risk comfort, and the real reason behind exploring these options.

\n

Only if the fit looks meaningful do we discuss whether AIF, PMS, or SIF belongs in the picture, and in what way. Our role is not to push complexity. Our role is to help clients understand whether these specialised routes actually make sense for them.

\n

The right question to ask

AIF, PMS, and SIF are not better just because they sound more exclusive. They are simply different investment routes meant for different kinds of investors and different kinds of needs.

\n

The important question is not whether they look attractive. The important question is whether they are suitable.
